# ArchLucid Future Packaging Enforcement Map

**Audience:** product, sales, architecture, and go-to-market stakeholders who need a practical view of how ArchLucid’s current layer model could evolve into stronger commercial packaging over time.

**Status:** Future-state packaging guidance. This document does **not** implement licensing, billing, or entitlement. It explains how today’s layer model can support future commercial discipline.

**Related:** [PRODUCT_PACKAGING.md](PRODUCT_PACKAGING.md) · [OPERATOR_DECISION_GUIDE.md](OPERATOR_DECISION_GUIDE.md) · [PILOT_ROI_MODEL.md](PILOT_ROI_MODEL.md) · [README.md](../README.md)

---

## 1. Why this document exists

ArchLucid already has a clear three-layer model:

- **Core Pilot**
- **Advanced Analysis**
- **Enterprise Controls**

That model is useful today for buyer clarity, operator guidance, and progressive disclosure.

The remaining question is how those same boundaries could evolve into stronger commercial packaging later without creating confusion or premature complexity.

---

## 2. Packaging today

Today, the layer model works in four ways:

### 2.1 Narrative packaging

The layers explain the product clearly:

- **Core Pilot** = first useful architecture result
- **Advanced Analysis** = deeper comparison and investigation
- **Enterprise Controls** = governance, auditability, compliance, and trust operations

### 2.2 UI visibility shaping

The operator shell uses progressive disclosure so users do not see the entire product by default.

### 2.3 Role-shaped usage

Some surfaces are naturally better suited to operators or admins, especially inside Enterprise Controls.

### 2.4 Commercial foundation

The same layer model can later support pricing, plan differentiation, or entitlement if needed.

---

## 3. Packaging later

A practical future commercialization path would distinguish between four kinds of boundaries.

### 3.1 Narrative-only boundaries

Some distinctions should remain mostly explanatory rather than enforced.

Good candidates:

- the conceptual difference between first-pilot work and deeper design investigation,
- guidance about when a team should stay narrow versus expand,
- role guidance for pilot operators versus governance stakeholders.

These help buyers and operators but do not necessarily need hard enforcement.

### 3.2 Visibility-based boundaries

Some capabilities can remain in the same product but be disclosed only when needed.

Good candidates:

- compare,
- replay,
- graph,
- pilot feedback,
- planning and evolution views,
- advanced alert and governance surfaces.

These are good fits for UI shaping because they reduce cognitive load without requiring commercial gating immediately.

### 3.3 Role-based boundaries

Some capabilities naturally align to role and responsibility more than plan.

Good candidates:

- governance approvals,
- policy packs,
- audit log access,
- alert tuning and routing,
- governance dashboard,
- operator/admin trust surfaces.

These are sensible as role-aware capabilities because they are tied to operational accountability.

### 3.4 Plan-based or entitlement-based boundaries

Some capabilities are plausible candidates for future commercial packaging if the product moves toward harder monetization boundaries.

Good candidates:

- advanced replay and comparison depth,
- graph and provenance analysis depth,
- advisory and recommendation-learning surfaces,
- advanced governance analytics,
- audit export depth and historical retention options,
- trial enforcement and conversion-related surfaces.

These should only become plan-gated when the customer value and packaging strategy are mature enough to justify it.

---

## 4. What should probably remain in the base product

The following should likely remain part of a strong base/Core Pilot offer because they define the first successful buying motion:

- create request,
- execute run,
- commit manifest,
- review artifacts,
- basic export capability,
- basic health and supportability,
- basic trustable operator flow.

If these are weakened too much, the wedge becomes harder to sell.

---

## 5. What should not be commercialized too early

Avoid hard-commercializing these too early:

- basic operator usability,
- the minimum reviewable-output flow,
- the basic ROI proof path,
- the basic evidence story required to trust the pilot.

If you commercialize these too aggressively, you risk damaging adoption before the wedge is fully established.

---

## 6. Practical future-state map

| Boundary type | What it means | Good examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Narrative-only** | Explained differently, but not enforced | Core Pilot vs deeper design investigation |
| **Visibility-based** | Hidden until relevant | Compare, Replay, Graph, planning surfaces |
| **Role-based** | Shaped by responsibility | Governance approvals, audit log, policy packs |
| **Plan-based** | Candidate for future pricing or entitlement | Advanced analytics depth, governance analytics, retention/export depth |

---

## 7. What is intentionally undecided

This document does **not** decide:

- exact pricing tiers,
- exact entitlement logic,
- separate binaries or deployment artifacts,
- final plan names,
- final billing enforcement.

Those decisions should follow stronger product-market evidence, not precede it.

---

## 8. Recommended rule for future commercialization

Use this rule:

> **Do not hard-gate a capability unless it is both commercially legible and non-essential to proving the first successful product outcome.**

That keeps monetization from damaging the wedge.

---

## 9. Summary

ArchLucid already has a strong packaging model. The next step is not to rush into a licensing engine. The next step is to preserve clarity about which boundaries are:

- explanatory,
- visibility-based,
- role-based,
- or likely to become commercial later.

That gives the product room to mature without losing discipline.
